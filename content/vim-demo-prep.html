5. Oron's Likely Questions â€” Prep Your Answers

How do you get wearable data from patients? They're not going to install another app.
We don't need them to. Serif connects through the device APIs directly â€” Apple HealthKit, Google Health Connect, Garmin, Oura, WHOOP. Many patients already wear these. The daemon runs server-side on consented data streams. Serif handles the data pipeline; Vim handles the consent flow. Patients opt in once through the care portal, and the data flows.
What about HIPAA? Wearable data isn't covered by HIPAA â€” how do you handle that?
You're right â€” wearable data in isolation isn't PHI. But the moment we link it to a clinical identity and surface it in the EHR through a Vim Card, it becomes part of the clinical record and falls under Vim's existing HIPAA framework. Serif processes data in a BAA-compliant environment. The patient consents to share device data for clinical use. We've already navigated this in our Human Edge deployment.
What percentage of patients actually wear devices? Is this a niche signal?
One in three American adults wears a smartwatch or fitness tracker â€” that's over 80 million people and growing 15% annually. For chronic conditions like hypertension and diabetes, wearable adoption is even higher because patients are motivated. And with Apple Watch and Samsung rolling out medical-grade sensors (blood pressure, ECG, SpO2), this is going from niche to standard of care. The question isn't if this data matters â€” it's who gets it to the provider first.
How is this different from what Apple or Google are building with their health platforms?
Apple and Google are building consumer-facing health features. They're not delivering clinical intelligence into EHR workflows. They don't have the provider relationship, the EHR integration, or the payer alignment. Vim does. Serif is the translation layer between what Apple/Google collect and what a provider needs at point of care. We're complementary to those platforms, not competitive. We're actually their best path into the clinical workflow â€” through Vim.
What's the validation? Is this clinically proven?
The individual signal types â€” HRV for cardiovascular risk, sleep architecture for metabolic health, circadian rhythm for mood disorders â€” these are all published and peer-reviewed. What Serif adds is the fusion and the causal inference: combining multiple signals into a clinical-grade insight with confidence intervals. In our Human Edge pilot, we reduced clinician analysis time from 80 hours to 10 minutes per patient while improving patient engagement 3.2Ã—. We're not inventing new medicine â€” we're automating what the best clinicians already do, at scale.
Why should Vim build this with you instead of building it in-house?
Three reasons. One: the causal inference engine took 18+ months to build â€” it's not a weekend project. Hierarchical Bayesian models, auto-causal DAG generation, temporal signal fusion across different data frequencies. Two: you'd be pulling engineers off your core middleware platform, which is where your competitive advantage is. Three: Vim's moat is the EHR integration and provider network. Serif's moat is the health intelligence engine. Partnership is faster and cleaner than build. And if the POC doesn't deliver, you've invested nothing.
What does this cost? What's the business model?
Flexible. We can structure as per-provider-per-month, per-card-impression, or a flat platform fee during pilot. For the POC, I'm proposing zero cost to Vim â€” I'm investing my side to prove the value. Post-POC, we'd work out unit economics based on what Vim charges health plans. Our pilot pricing with other partners has been in the $1-2 per user per month range, which is a fraction of the value-based care savings from even one avoided hospitalization.
How do you handle data quality issues? Wearable data is noisy.
It is. That's exactly why you need a causal inference engine rather than raw threshold alerts. The daemon uses Bayesian methods specifically because they handle noise well â€” they express uncertainty as confidence intervals rather than false precision. If the data is low confidence, the card says so. A provider seeing "78% confidence: autonomic shift indicating elevated BP risk" is far more actionable than either a false-positive alert or no alert at all. The daemon also requires 14-60 days of continuous data before generating signals â€” it doesn't react to one bad night of sleep.
You mentioned Human Edge â€” that's a longevity clinic. How does this translate to mainstream healthcare?
Human Edge was our proof-of-concept: high-touch, data-rich, small scale. It validated that the engine works and that providers trust the output. The Vim integration is the scale play. The same intelligence engine, but instead of powering a boutique clinic dashboard, it's powering Vim Cards across 150,000 providers. The algorithm doesn't change â€” the delivery mechanism does. And Vim is the delivery mechanism that actually reaches mainstream healthcare.
